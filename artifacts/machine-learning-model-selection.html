Decision Principle

The most advanced model is not always the best model

A strong machine learning recommendation begins with the data and the decision environment. Structured tables, raw images, real-time constraints, explanation requirements, and the cost of failure create very different technical needs. The appropriate approach is the simplest one that can learn the required signal, satisfy the business objective, and operate within an acceptable risk boundary.

Case 01 · Traditional ML

California House-Price Prediction

A real-estate team needs repeatable value estimates from location, square footage, room counts, property age, and neighborhood conditions.

Why it fits
  • The inputs are structured, tabular features.
  • Regression and tree-based models provide strong, efficient baselines.
  • Predictions can be evaluated and explained to clients more directly.
Why deep learning is not first choice

Neural networks add data, compute, tuning, and explainability costs without a clear baseline advantage. They become more relevant only if the scope expands to images, floor plans, satellite data, or large volumes of listing text.

Case 02 · Deep Learning

Autonomous Taxi Perception

An autonomous vehicle must interpret pedestrians, vehicles, signals, lanes, and obstacles from continuous camera, lidar, radar, and other sensor streams.

Why it fits
  • Neural networks learn visual, spatial, and temporal representations.
  • The approach can support detection, segmentation, prediction, and sensor fusion.
  • Large-scale data can cover patterns that handcrafted rules cannot enumerate.
Why traditional ML alone is insufficient

Conventional models can support narrow tasks such as travel-time forecasting, but they cannot independently interpret raw, high-dimensional road scenes at the level required for a complete perception system.

Client Decision Lens

Five questions before choosing a model

  1. Data: Is it structured, unstructured, multimodal, or sequential?
  2. Outcome: What decision will the prediction support?
  3. Explainability: Who must understand or challenge the result?
  4. Operations: What latency, compute, and maintenance can the organization support?
  5. Risk: What happens when the system is wrong?
